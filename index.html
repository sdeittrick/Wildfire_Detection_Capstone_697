The New Way To Prevent Wildfires

Protecting the Forest.
In recent years, climate change has facilitated an inexcusable influx of large-scale droughts and lengthy fire seasons across the globe. Disturbingly the dramatic increase in wildfires has devastated communities and natural beauty. In California, during the record drought conditions of 2014, Fire Safe Marin, Pacific Gas and Electric Company (PG&E), and Marin County Fire Department collaborated to install Marin’s remote wildfire detection system. One of the first systems served to assist firefighters in identifying new wildfire locations. Since then, the operations had expanded in 2017. The Geographic community has pressured local and federal governments to implement human-monitored camera systems to detect forest fires.
While there is a wide range of methods in monitoring forests, only recently have methods of virtual segmentation datasets for image recognition that can realistically simulate large-scale tree canopies been available. This research avenue has expanded due to advancements in real-time rendering through advanced graphics cards capable of light particle simulation with ray tracing and virtual geometry systems such as Nanite available through Unreal Engine 5. Together these methods provide a realistic environment virtually that can replace the needs for real-life image data collection.
Developing an Unreal Dataset.
Using Unreal Engine for dataset generation can be plug-and-play with Blueprints and landscape procedural generation. Though there is significant research and design in generating accurate data, the core methods are simplistic. They are essentially teleporting a first-person controller around the map to take screenshots of the landscape to create a robust dataset.
Though for this project, we needed variation in the environment. We used a plugin for Unreal Engine called Sky Creator that could dynamically change the time of day and cloud coverage. By creating small datasets of minor variations with these differing conditions, we combined them into a large dataset that represents the landscape with typical cloud coverages and lighting conditions.

The dataset we created is a 35 x 35 kilometers map based on real-life topography outside Nimrod, Oregon, in the Willamette National Forest. Known for their beautiful dense Douglass Fir forests, these forests can easily be replicated in-game through procedural generation by creating parameters between various assets. Though processing power is a limiting factor, using Unreal console commands can help get clear screenshots.
Of course, not every landscape looks like the one we generated. But that is precisely the reason we chose to use Unreal Engine to create a custom dataset. Suppose we included another geographic area like the Western Rocky Mountains or the Australian Bush. In that case, we can get real-life topography and use procedural generation precisely like I did to populate the landscape with foliage.
Data Processing and Modeling.
Processing that images required transforming them into a format that could be input into our TensorFlow CNN model. This included normalizing pixels to values between 0 and 1, converting to arrays, and converting to tensors. An additional processing measure, data augmentation, was conducted during model development to prevent overfitting, thus theoretically improving generalization. Specifically, functionality was built to flip images along the vertical access, increase contrast, increase saturation, and increase brightness. Ultimately, only flipping images provided predictive improvement. We anticipate the marginal benefit came from a larger sample size of essentially the same pictures.
Model development involved tinkering with layers and hyperparameters along the way. Specifically, we adjusted the number of convolution layers, activation functions, and inclusions of dropout layers. Additionally, various units, dropout rates, and optimizers were iteratively assessed during model generation and prediction. Performance dictated our direction with changes made to the model over time.
Measuring Performance.
Improving our model’s performance proved challenging, given both the limited availability of images and the ideal theoretical testing split for images with fire and those without. An extreme testing split was suggested to us based on the premise that the vast majority of images incoming through an active video stream will not contain the fire. We used a confusion matrix and AUC to guide our hyperparameter tuning as we worked through this. We also leveraged both real-world datasets and unreal engine images to assess generalization in testing. The disparity in performance between the two raises several questions which warrant additional research. First, are the Unreal Engine images we’ve trained the model on fundamentally different than real-world images in terms of “realness”? Or, do the real-world images capture scenes that differ substantially from those in unreal. Based on our qualitative review, we suppose the latter. More real-world images similar in the scene to those produced in Unreal Engine are needed to test this hypothesis.
The Future of Unreal Datasets.
This type of dataset generation is extremely new because it wasn't quite possible in the past. But as graphical technologies dramatically improve, the line of real-life and hyper-realism is blurred. Today the latest iterations of Unreal Engine come closer than we ever have in real-time to replicating life, and indeed, with careful calibration, can look real life to a neural network.
Though virtual datasets for image segmentation is not foolproof and needs careful consideration for the environment it represents, it certainly has a future in climate science in the greater data science fields. Our hope for this project is to add to the research of such datasets and provide input on how future developments can improve with our findings.
